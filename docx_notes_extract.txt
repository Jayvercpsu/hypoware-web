[128] 1\ufe0f\u20e3 Notice Symptoms Early
[129] Pay attention to early warning signs such as:
[130] Shakiness
[131] Sweating
[132] Dizziness
[133] Sudden hunger
[134] Fast heartbeat
[135] Confusion or irritability
[136] Recognizing symptoms early helps prevent the condition from becoming severe.
[137] 2\ufe0f\u20e3 Check Blood Sugar (If Possible)
[138] If a glucometer is available, check your blood glucose level immediately to confirm hypoglycemia (usually below 70 mg/dL). If checking is not possible but symptoms are present, treat it right away.
[139] 3\ufe0f\u20e3 Take Quick-Acting Sugar Immediately
[140] Consume15 grams of fast-acting carbohydrates, such as:
[141] 3\u20134 glucose tablets
[142] \xbd cup regular (non-diet) soda
[143] \xbd cup fruit juice
[144] 1 tablespoon of sugar or honey dissolved in water
[145] Avoid chocolate or fatty foods at this stage because they slow down sugar absorption.
[146] 4\ufe0f\u20e3 Wait 15 Minutes and Recheck
[147] Wait 15 minutes, then:
[148] Recheck your blood sugar level.
[149] If it is still below normal or symptoms continue, repeat the 15 grams of fast-acting sugar.
[150] This is known as the15\u201315 Rule.
[151] 5\ufe0f\u20e3 Eat a Carb + Protein Follow-Up Snack
[152] Once blood sugar returns to normal, eat a balanced snack that includes:
[153] Carbohydrates (e.g., crackers, bread, fruit)
[154] Protein (e.g., peanut butter, cheese, egg)
[155] This helps stabilize blood sugar and prevents another drop.
[156] 6\ufe0f\u20e3 Rest and Avoid Strenuous Activity
[157] Sit or lie down while recovering. Avoid exercise or physically demanding tasks until blood sugar levels are stable.
[158] 7\ufe0f\u20e3 Inform Someone Nearby
[159] If symptoms are severe or worsening, inform a family member, friend, teacher, or coworker so they can assist if needed.
[160] 8\ufe0f\u20e3 Seek Medical Help if Severe
[161] If the person becomes unconscious, has seizures, or cannot swallow:
[162] Do NOT give food or drinks by mouth.
[163] A trained person may administer glucagon if available.
[164] Call emergency medical services immediately.

[170] 1\ufe0f\u20e3 Carbohydrates \u2013 Main Source of Energy
[171] Carbs give your body glucose, which is your brain and body\u2019s primary fuel source. For people prone to low blood sugar, balanced carbs help prevent sudden drops.
[172] Examples:
[173] Rice (white or brown)
[174] Whole wheat bread
[175] Oatmeal
[176] Sweet potatoes
[177] Corn
[178] Fruits (banana, apple, mango, orange)
[179] Why it\u2019s important:Provides quick and steady energy. Choosing complex carbs (brown rice, oats, whole wheat) helps release sugar slowly into the bloodstream.
[180] 2\ufe0f\u20e3 Protein \u2013 Stabilizes Blood Sugar
[181] Protein slows down digestion and helps prevent rapid spikes and drops in blood sugar levels.
[182] Examples:
[183] Eggs
[184] Fish (tilapia, tuna, salmon)
[185] Chicken breast
[186] Lean pork or beef
[187] Tofu
[188] Beans and lentils
[189] Greek yogurt
[190] Why it\u2019s important:Keeps you full longer and supports muscle repair and body functions.
[191] 3\ufe0f\u20e3 Healthy Fats \u2013 Longer-Lasting Energy
[192] Healthy fats slow glucose absorption and help maintain stable energy levels.
[193] Examples:
[194] Nuts (almonds, peanuts, cashews)
[195] Avocado
[196] Milk
[197] Cheese
[198] Olive oil
[199] Chia seeds
[200] Why it\u2019s important:Provides sustained energy and supports heart and brain health.
